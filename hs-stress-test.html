The Interpretation Gap

Inside the organisation, safety governance feels coherent. Policies exist. Training runs. Hazards are managed. Leadership is engaged.

After an incident, none of that matters in the way you expect. Investigators reconstruct your decisions from documents, meeting minutes, and the absence of documents. They do not assess intentions. They read signals.

The distance between how you experience your governance and how a regulator reconstructs it is where personal liability lives. Most directors discover that distance only after something forces the question.

What the Stress Test Examines

Six structural areas. Each one shapes how your H&S governance reads under scrutiny.

Policy Architecture

Whether your documented policies reflect operational reality — or exist independently of it.

Risk Registers

Whether identified risks are actively governed or the register has become a static artefact.

Incident Records

What the pattern of recorded incidents reveals about oversight — and what the absences reveal.

Training & Competency

Whether records demonstrate genuine capability or procedural compliance.

Governance Minutes

Whether H&S appears in board records as active oversight — or only after something goes wrong.

Officer Duty Alignment

Whether the governance trail supports a credible due diligence defence under HSWA Section 44.

What You Receive

A structured exposure report. One document. Fixed scope.

  • Evidence that stands on its own — documented, verifiable, defensible
  • Gaps that would require verbal explanation under investigation
  • Ambiguity in the governance trail that an investigator would interpret, not ignore
  • The likely consolidation path for accountability after a serious event
  • The specific points where internal experience and external interpretation diverge

Who This Is For

Directors and officers in operational businesses who have never seen their safety governance through an investigator's lens. Leaders whose systems feel adequate — but who have never tested what adequate looks like when someone else tells the story.

Under HSWA, officer duties are personal. They do not transfer to a safety manager. They do not disappear into a policy manual. If something goes wrong, the question is what you did — and what the documents prove you did.

Why Now

The Gibson decision confirmed that directors face personal prosecution — not just the company. The HSWA Amendment Bill introduced in February 2026 signals further tightening. The regulatory direction is not ambiguous.

The question is not whether scrutiny will arrive. The question is whether the governance trail will hold when it does.

Most directors will not test that until an incident forces it.
