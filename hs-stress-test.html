The Interpretation Gap

Most directors experience their safety governance as active and engaged. Policies exist. Training occurs. Hazards are managed. The business feels safe.

After an incident, investigators do not assess intentions. They reconstruct decisions from documents, meeting minutes, and the absence of documents. The gap between how you experience your governance and how an investigator reconstructs it is where personal liability lives.

The Health & Safety Stress Test reveals that gap before an incident writes the story for you.

What the Stress Test Examines

Six structural areas that determine how your H&S governance would be interpreted under scrutiny.

Policy Architecture

Whether documented policies reflect actual operational practice — or exist independently of it.

Risk Registers

Whether identified risks are actively governed, or the register has become a static compliance artefact.

Incident & Near-Miss Records

What the pattern of recorded incidents reveals about oversight quality — and what the gaps reveal.

Training & Competency

Whether training records demonstrate genuine capability development or procedural box-ticking.

Governance Minutes

Whether board and management records show active safety oversight — or H&S appears only after something goes wrong.

Officer Duty Alignment

Whether the documented governance trail supports a credible due diligence defence under HSWA Section 44.

What You Receive

A structured exposure report. One document. Fixed scope.

  • What can be clearly evidenced from existing documentation
  • What would rely on verbal explanation rather than documented proof
  • Where ambiguity exists in the governance trail
  • How accountability would likely consolidate under investigation
  • Where the gap between internal experience and external interpretation is widest

Who This Is For

Owner-directors and officers who carry personal liability under the Health and Safety at Work Act. Leaders who want to understand how their governance would read under scrutiny — before scrutiny arrives.

Why Now

The enforcement environment is shifting. The Gibson decision demonstrated that directors face personal prosecution under HSWA — not just the company. Officer duties under Section 44 carry individual liability that cannot be delegated to a safety manager or outsourced to a consultant.

The HSWA Amendment Bill introduced in February 2026 signals further tightening. The regulatory direction is clear. The question for directors is not whether scrutiny will arrive — but whether the governance trail will hold when it does.

Most directors will not test this until after an incident forces the question.
